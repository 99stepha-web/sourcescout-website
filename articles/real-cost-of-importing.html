Understanding the real cost of importing a product

A product that appears inexpensive at the supplier's factory can cost significantly more by the time it reaches your warehouse or front door. Understanding landed cost helps you compare sourcing opportunities more realistically.

SourceScout Guide Updated July 2026 7 min read
Important: Import taxes, customs procedures and fees vary by country, product and shipment. This guide provides a general framework, not country-specific customs or tax advice.

Product price is only the starting point

New importers often compare suppliers using only the price shown on a quotation or marketplace listing. That can produce a misleading comparison.

The more useful figure is often the landed cost: the total cost required to purchase the goods and bring them to the location where you can actually use or sell them.

1. The cost of the goods

Start with the actual amount payable for the products. Check whether the quotation includes customization, accessories, spare parts or special packaging.

Minimum order quantities can also affect your real investment. A lower unit price may require purchasing considerably more inventory.

2. Packaging and preparation

Export packaging may be charged separately, particularly for machinery, fragile goods or customized retail packaging.

Ask whether cartons, pallets, wooden cases or other export preparation are included in the quotation.

3. Transportation before international shipping

Your goods may need to travel from the supplier's facility to a warehouse, consolidation point, airport or seaport before the international journey even begins.

Depending on the agreed shipping terms, these costs may be included in the supplier's quotation or charged separately.

4. International freight

International transportation can represent a significant part of the final cost, particularly for large, heavy or low-value products.

Common transportation methods include courier services, air freight and sea freight. The cheapest method per kilogram is not automatically the best option when transit time, shipment size and destination charges are considered.

PRACTICAL TIP

Compare products using landed cost, not factory price.

A $5 product with expensive transportation can ultimately cost more than a $7 alternative that packs and ships more efficiently.

5. Insurance

Depending on the shipment and shipping arrangement, cargo insurance may be an additional cost worth considering.

Understand what risks are covered, the insured value and the procedure for making a claim.

6. Customs duties and taxes

Imported goods may be subject to customs duties, value-added taxes and other government charges.

The amount can depend on the product classification, declared customs value, country of origin and destination country's rules.

For commercial shipments, confirm the applicable requirements with an appropriate customs professional or government authority.

7. Customs clearance and destination charges

A shipment arriving in your country does not necessarily mean all transportation costs have been paid.

Depending on the shipping arrangement, there may be clearance, handling, documentation, storage, terminal or delivery charges.

Ask your freight provider what is included before approving the shipment.

8. Final delivery

Include the cost of moving the goods from the port, airport, courier facility or warehouse to their final destination.

For bulky equipment, unloading or specialized transportation may also need to be considered.

A simple landed-cost framework

✓ Product cost
✓ Packaging
✓ Origin transportation
✓ International freight
✓ Cargo insurance
✓ Duties and taxes
✓ Customs and handling charges
✓ Final delivery

Calculate cost per sellable unit

Once you estimate the complete landed cost, divide it by the number of units you realistically expect to receive in sellable condition.

Businesses may also need to account for damaged units, payment fees, warehousing and other operating expenses when determining whether a product can be sold profitably.

Final thought

The lowest supplier quotation does not always produce the lowest final cost. Understanding the complete journey from supplier to destination makes product and supplier comparisons much more useful.
